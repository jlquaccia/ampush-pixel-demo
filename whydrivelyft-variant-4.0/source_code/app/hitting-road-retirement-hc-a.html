Hitting the Road After Retirement

  • I had been retired for just shy of three years when an old work buddy referred me to Lyft. I had been enjoying my retirement for the most part. I had my freedom with a car, a couple of volunteer projects and enjoyed spending time with family, but I still wasn’t filling my schedule like a full-time job used to, and I found myself getting a bit bored. I didn’t want to go back to work, but I didn’t want to sit at home all day either.

  • Lyft was a great solution for me. I already had a smartphone to keep in touch with the family, but aside from making calls and sending the occasional text message, I can’t say I’m the most savvy user. I appreciated that getting set up to drive was very easy—I just had to open the app and put my details in, and there were no complicated steps or technical lingo to deal with. They’ll even help you get a smartphone if you don’t have one already!

  • I’ve always been a very early riser—usually up around five or six AM. Lyft has been a real blessing in that sense, as it gives me something fun and productive to do in those early hours. It’s not the biggest issue for me, but those hours are also where I’ve found the most money to be made. I’m doing a lot of airport runs for business trips, ferrying men and women to offices and breakfast meetings, a lot of ‘company time’ stuff.

  • At first I was surprised at just how respectful and polite my passengers were — I’m told to ‘have a great day’ fifteen, twenty times over! Passengers are often interested in what I used to do for work and I’ve had some great chats with younger people working their way up in the same industry. Like I said, it’s not about the money for me — although it’s a nice perk to charge a great dinner out or ticket to something to Lyft. Even if I just do one morning per week (if I’m feeling particularly energetic I’ve been known to do all five weekday mornings) it’s a great way to stay active and productive, and to meet new folks of all ages!
